# The GTM Motion That Compounds (And Why Most Series A Companies Don't Have One)

Here's a scene I've watched play out six times in the last two years.

A Series A company raises $8–12M. The board wants pipeline growth. The CEO hires a VP of Marketing, maybe a couple of SDRs, stands up a paid program, launches a content calendar. Six months later, they're spending $40K/month on demand gen and can't explain where the pipeline is coming from — or why it isn't compounding.

The board meeting goes something like: "We need more leads." So they spend more. CAC goes up. Pipeline stays flat. The VP of Marketing gets replaced. The cycle restarts.

I've sat in that board meeting. I've been the person brought in after the second or third VP of Marketing. And the problem is almost never the people. It's that nobody designed a growth system. They assembled a collection of tactics and hoped momentum would show up on its own.

It won't. Momentum is engineered or it doesn't exist.

## The physics underneath your GTM

I use a framework called the Physics of Growth to diagnose what's actually happening inside a company's go-to-market system. It's not complicated, but it explains patterns I see over and over that spreadsheet analysis misses.

Three forces matter: **Momentum**, **Friction**, and **Gravity**.

**Momentum** is the compounding force. It's what happens when your growth investments build on each other instead of starting from zero every quarter. A company with momentum has declining CAC, increasing organic pipeline share, content that generates leads six months after publication, and a brand that buyers recognize before the first sales call.

A company without momentum is running on a treadmill. Every dollar invested yields less than the last. You're buying pipeline instead of building it. The tell is when "we need more leads" is a permanent state of affairs no matter how much you spend.

**Friction** is the hidden destroyer. It's everything in your buyer's path that slows them down, confuses them, or gives them a reason to go do something else. I'm not just talking about a bad form or a slow website. I'm talking about the entire experience of doing business with you.

Last year I audited a Series B company's buyer journey. From first touch to signed contract, a buyer had to navigate 14 distinct steps — including three that existed solely because of an internal handoff nobody had questioned in two years. They were losing 60% of qualified prospects in the middle of their funnel and blaming "lead quality." The leads were fine. The process was punishing.

Friction compounds too, just in the wrong direction. Every bad experience trains the market to avoid you. The prospect who bounced from your qualification gauntlet told three peers about it. That cost never shows up in your attribution dashboard.

**Gravity** is the organic pull that attracts buyers to you without you pushing. It's built from genuine authority — the accumulated weight of your expertise, your point of view, your presence in the places where buyers are already looking for answers. Companies with strong gravity get inbound that "just happens." Companies without it are always chasing.

In 2026, gravity increasingly means being the answer when someone asks an AI about your category. Not a link in a search result — the actual answer. If ChatGPT or Perplexity can't describe what your company does and why it matters, you have a gravity problem that no amount of ad spend will fix.

## Why Series A companies get this wrong

The mistake happens early and it's structural.

Most Series A GTM plans are built around tactics, not systems. The plan says: hire SDRs, run paid campaigns, publish content, attend events. Each tactic gets a budget and a target. Nobody asks whether these things connect into something that compounds.

Tactics without a system is like assembling car parts without a blueprint. You might have an engine, wheels, and a steering column, but if nobody designed how they work together, you don't have a car. You have an expensive pile of components.

The compounding GTM motion requires three things most early-stage companies skip:

**A positioning foundation that's actually load-bearing.** Not a tagline exercise. A clear, defensible point of view about who you serve, what problem you solve better than anyone, and why now. Everything else is built on this. If it's weak, nothing on top of it compounds.

**Intentional friction reduction.** Map the buyer journey. Every step. Ask who each step serves — the buyer or your internal process. Most companies find 30-40% of their funnel exists for internal reasons. Cut it. The compound effect of a frictionless buying experience is one of the most underrated growth levers in B2B.

**Sustained investment in gravity.** This means building genuine topical authority in your category through consistent, structured thought leadership — not a blog post every other week, but a deliberate content architecture that makes you the definitive source on the problems you solve. This is slow. It compounds. And it's the only thing that builds durable pipeline that doesn't disappear when you turn off the ad budget.

## The question worth asking

If you stopped all paid spend tomorrow, what would happen to your pipeline in 90 days?

If the answer is "it would collapse," you don't have a GTM motion that compounds. You have a rented pipeline. And rented pipeline is a fine way to get started, but it's a terrible place to be at Series B when the board wants to see efficient growth.

The good news: this is fixable. It requires designing a system, not just running more plays.
