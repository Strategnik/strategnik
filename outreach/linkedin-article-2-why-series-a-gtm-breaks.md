# Why Every Series A GTM Leader Fails in the Same Way

I watched it happen again last month.

A Series A startup -- solid product, $8M raised, real customers paying real money -- hired a VP of Marketing. Let's call her Sarah. Sarah had 12 years of experience, a strong track record at two well-known B2B companies, and genuine enthusiasm for the product. The board was thrilled. The CEO posted about it on LinkedIn. "Excited to welcome our first marketing leader."

Sarah lasted nine months.

Not because she was bad. Because she walked into a building with no foundation and was told to install the roof.

There was no defined ICP beyond "companies that might buy this." No positioning document. No clarity on whether the product-led motion or the sales-led motion was primary. No agreement between the CEO and the head of sales on what a qualified lead even meant. The CRM was a graveyard of untagged contacts. And the board wanted pipeline numbers in 90 days.

Sarah did what any competent marketing leader would do: she tried to build the plane while flying it. She ran campaigns. She hired an SDR. She launched a webinar series. She built a website refresh proposal. She started seven things simultaneously because everything felt urgent and nothing had been prioritized.

By month four, the CEO was frustrated that pipeline hadn't materialized. By month six, the board was asking pointed questions. By month nine, Sarah and the company "mutually agreed to part ways." The LinkedIn post was quieter this time.

I've seen this exact movie play out at least 30 times. The VP of Sales version. The VP of Marketing version. The "Head of Growth" version. The details change. The ending doesn't.

## The stat nobody talks about

The average tenure of a VP of Sales at a Series A company is roughly 7 months. VP of Marketing is slightly longer, but not by much. These aren't bad hires. Most of them are experienced operators who've done this work successfully at companies with the infrastructure to support it.

The problem isn't the person. The problem is the sequence.

## The pattern

Here's what almost every post-Series A company does:

1. Raise money
2. Feel pressure to show growth
3. Hire a senior GTM leader to "own" growth
4. Expect that leader to simultaneously build the strategy, the systems, the team, and the pipeline
5. Get impatient when it doesn't produce results in one quarter
6. Part ways with the hire
7. Repeat

It's a $300K-$500K mistake when you factor in comp, recruiting fees, ramp time, and opportunity cost. And most companies make it at least twice before they realize the issue isn't hiring. The issue is what's missing before the hire.

## What's actually missing

A GTM leader needs a system to operate within. Not a perfect system -- but the bones of one. That means:

**A real ICP.** Not "mid-market SaaS companies." An actual, evidence-based description of who your best customers are, what triggers them to buy, and why they choose you over the alternative (which is usually doing nothing).

**A positioning thesis.** What are you, who is it for, and why should they care? If the CEO, the head of product, and the first sales rep all give different answers to that question, you don't have positioning. You have opinions.

**A pipeline architecture.** How do leads come in? What happens to them? Where does marketing end and sales begin? What does "qualified" mean in specific, measurable terms? If nobody has decided this, your new VP isn't going to build pipeline. They're going to spend four months building the plumbing that should have existed before they arrived.

**Founder-market clarity.** At Series A, the founder is still the most credible seller and the most important signal of what the company is. A GTM leader can amplify that signal. They can't create it from scratch. If the founder hasn't done the hard work of understanding the buying motion -- who says yes, who says no, what the objections are, what the sales cycle looks like -- no hire is going to figure that out for them.

## Why founders get this wrong

It's not stupidity. It's pattern-matching from the wrong reference class.

Founders look at Series B and C companies and see VP-level GTM leaders running big motions. They assume the leader is what created the motion. But in most cases, the motion was built first -- often by the founders themselves, sometimes with outside help -- and the VP was brought in to scale what already worked.

Hiring a GTM leader to figure out your go-to-market is like hiring a general contractor to also be the architect. Some people can do both. But you're making the job dramatically harder and the probability of success dramatically lower.

The other factor is board pressure. Investors want to see a "real team" in place. A VP of Sales on the org chart signals maturity. But hiring for optics instead of readiness is how you burn $400K and end up right back where you started, six months later, with less runway and more scar tissue.

## The uncomfortable truth

If you're a Series A founder and you don't yet have a clear ICP, a defensible positioning story, and a basic understanding of your pipeline mechanics -- you're not ready to hire a full-time GTM leader. You need to build the system first, then hire someone to run it.

That might mean doing the work yourself. It might mean bringing in outside help on a focused, time-bound basis to get the fundamentals in place. It might mean hiring a more junior person who can execute against a defined playbook rather than a senior leader who needs a blank canvas and six months of runway to prove themselves.

Whatever the path, the sequence matters more than the talent. The best VP of Marketing in the world will fail if you hand them chaos and a 90-day clock.
